# Earnings Quality Red Flag Cases

## Case 1: TSLA, margin sensitivity and narrative risk

Pattern tags:
- `earnings-quality`
- `narrative-vs-economics`

Context:
- TSLA trades with a large narrative premium relative to conventional valuation anchors

Why it matters:
- when valuation is rich, even modest questions about margin durability, volume quality, or earnings consistency become amplified

Operational takeaway:
- in premium-priced names, earnings quality has to be judged more strictly, not less

Numbers that justify caution:
- gross margin around `19.1%`
- operating margin around `4.2%`
- profit margin around `3.9%`

Why that matters:
- at very high valuation, low-single-digit net profitability leaves little room for execution slippage

## Case 2: AVGO, adjusted success vs financing reality

Pattern tags:
- `earnings-quality`
- `balance-sheet-risk`

Context:
- Broadcom can show excellent adjusted operating performance
- investors still need to read that performance alongside leverage, integration, and capital structure

Operational takeaway:
- adjusted strength is useful, but fundamental quality must also survive the balance-sheet lens

Concrete takeaway:
- gross and operating margins are elite
- but debt near `64.9B` means the investor should always read quality together with financing structure and acquisition history

## Case 3: AAPL as a counterexample

Pattern tags:
- `earnings-quality`
- `high-quality-compounder`

Context:
- AAPL’s value as a case is partly that it teaches what cleaner earnings quality looks like

Lesson:
- when revenue, margins, buybacks, and cash generation align, the analysis burden shifts more toward valuation and demand durability than toward accounting mistrust

Operational takeaway:
- casebooks need clean positive examples as much as red-flag examples

Positive-control metrics:
- operating margin about `32.3%`
- profit margin about `27.2%`
- free cash flow about `101.1B`

Why this matters:
- a casebook needs examples of what trustworthy earnings quality looks like, not just what bad quality looks like

## Case 4: NVDA as a high-quality earnings benchmark

Pattern tags:
- `earnings-quality`
- `growth-quality`

Context:
- NVDA currently combines very high growth with very high margins and strong cash flow

Why it is useful:
- it shows that not all rapid-growth stories require aggressive excuse-making around profitability

Operational takeaway:
- the stronger the real economics, the less analysis needs to rely on "adjusted narrative" language
