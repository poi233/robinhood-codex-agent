# Business Quality

## Start With the Engine

A business should be understood as a money-making system, not a ticker with a story.

Ask:

- what does it sell?
- who pays?
- why do customers stay?
- what makes demand durable or fragile?

## Quality Markers

Higher-quality businesses often have:

- pricing power
- recurring or repeat demand
- attractive incremental margins
- low need for rescue financing
- clear competitive advantages
- rational management behavior

Lower-quality businesses often show:

- commodity pricing
- demand pulled forward by promotions
- dependence on one product, customer, or cycle
- high reinvestment with weak returns
- narrative strength but weak economic evidence

## Moat Is Not a Buzzword

Useful moat questions:

- does the product save money, create revenue, or just feel optional?
- can customers switch easily?
- is scale genuinely valuable or just large?
- is the company structurally advantaged or temporarily fashionable?

## Practical Output

At the end, classify the business as:

- durable compounder
- good business in cyclical market
- decent product but weak economics
- fragile model with narrative dependence
